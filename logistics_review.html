Definition
What Logistics Actually Covers

Logistics is not simply "moving goods." It is the coordinated flow and storage of goods and related information — from source of supply, through production, all the way to the point of consumption.

Logistics = Materials Management + Distribution

Students often reduce logistics to transport and warehousing. In reality it includes inventory, packaging, information flows, coordination, and reverse logistics. The central insight of Chapters 1 & 2 is that logistics is a system, not a set of isolated tasks.

Materials Management
Inbound flow — purchasing, inbound transport, receiving, and managing materials before and during production.
Distribution
Outbound flow — finished goods transport, warehousing, order fulfilment, and delivery to the customer.
Common mistake: treating logistics as only transport and storage. Information and coordination are equally core.

Total Logistics Concept
Why TLC Matters

The Total Logistics Concept (TLC) states that every logistics decision must be evaluated from the perspective of the whole system, not just the department taking the action.

Local Improvement ≠ System Improvement

A change that looks like a cost saving in one area may create greater costs elsewhere — destroying overall value even while appearing efficient in isolation.

Classic text example — Packaging: Removing cardboard boxes cut packaging cost. But goods became harder to stack and store. Transport efficiency dropped. Total logistics cost increased.
TLC
Total Logistics Concept
The core argument: Logistics functions are interdependent. You cannot optimize them in isolation without risking the performance of the whole.
Every department decision has a ripple effect through the supply system.

Sub-Optimization
The Core Mistake: Sub-Optimization

One of the most common misunderstandings in logistics is assuming each function should be optimized on its own. TLC directly rejects this view.

Sub-optimization — improving one part while making the whole system worse — is the failure mode TLC is designed to prevent.

The right question is never "is this function as efficient as possible?" It is "does this decision improve total system performance?"

Trade-offs can occur at four levels:

Level 01
Within one function

e.g. faster picking vs. higher picker error rate

Level 02
Between functions

e.g. smaller batches reduce inventory but raise transport cost

Level 03
Between company depts

e.g. marketing wants more SKUs; logistics prefers fewer

Level 04
Between organizations

e.g. supplier lead times affect buyer's inventory needs

Planning Hierarchy
Planning Hierarchy
Strategic · Tactical · Operational
Strategic
Long-term structural decisions. Network design, depot configuration, service policy, outsourcing decisions, transport mode selection, major trade-off choices. These decisions are hard to reverse and shape the entire logistics system.
Tactical
Medium-term subsystem decisions. Number of vehicles, delivery schedule structures, storage methods, warehouse layouts, stock control procedures, and information system support. Turns strategy into operational capability.
Operational
Day-to-day execution. Receiving, order picking, replenishment, dispatch, documentation, returns handling, and routine performance control. This is where strategy meets reality.
The point students miss: Each level answers a different kind of logistics question. Memorizing the labels without understanding what question each level addresses is not sufficient.

Integrated Systems
MRP / DRP & JIT
Materials Planning Systems
MRP / DRP

MRP (Materials Requirements Planning) ensures that materials and components are available for production when needed — reducing both shortages and excess stock.

DRP (Distribution Requirements Planning) applies similar logic to finished goods inventory across the distribution network — synchronising stock levels with expected demand.

Both systems translate demand signals and time horizons into actionable schedules — ensuring the right materials arrive at the right time without holding more than necessary.
Waste Elimination Philosophy
Just-In-Time (JIT)

JIT aims to eliminate waste, reduce non-value-adding activity, and minimise excess inventory. Instead of holding large safety stocks, JIT depends on reliable timing, close coordination, and real-time visibility throughout the supply chain.

Do not treat these as isolated technical tools. MRP, DRP, and JIT matter because they support the broader logic of integration — they are expressions of the TLC principle applied to planning and execution.

Key Tension
Globalization vs Quick-Response & JIT

Globalization typically lengthens supply chains and adds complexity. This creates a direct structural tension with quick-response and JIT, which require short lead times and high reliability.

Lead Times
↑
Global sourcing adds weeks or months in transit. JIT needs days — sometimes hours.
Reliability
↓
Cross-border routes are exposed to port delays, customs, weather, and political risk. JIT requires near-perfect timing.
Visibility
↑↑
More nodes, carriers, and handoffs demand greater tracking. Information complexity rises sharply.
Network
✕
More transport options but also more points of failure. Network design becomes harder to optimise.
Most important chapter argument:

Globalization may reduce some input costs, but it actively works against responsiveness. Companies must choose — or manage the trade-off explicitly.
Quick-response systems require fast replenishment cycles, low inventory, and reliable partners — the opposite of what long global supply chains provide.

Competitive Logic
Logistics as a Financial & Competitive Choice

Impact on ROI

Profit Side

Service levels, transport efficiency, storage utilisation, labour productivity, inventory carrying cost

Capital Side

Inventory value, cash locked in order cycles, warehouses, fleet assets — all affect capital employed

Two broad competitive positions

Position A
Cost Leader

Logistics structured for efficiency, scale, and low unit cost. Sacrifices flexibility and speed where necessary.

Position B
Service Leader

Logistics structured for speed, reliability, and responsiveness. Accepts higher costs to win on service.

Key lesson:

Logistics structure must support the competitive position the company is actually trying to achieve. Trying to maximise both low cost and high service without a clear design usually results in weak performance on both.

Detailed Answer Guide
Key Answers
How to answer common exam questions
Q1 — Why must logistics be managed holistically?

Logistics elements are interdependent. A decision that reduces cost in one area — such as packaging or warehousing — may increase cost or reduce service quality elsewhere. TLC therefore evaluates total system performance rather than isolated departmental outcomes.

Q2 — Why does globalization conflict with JIT and quick-response?

Global operations involve longer distances, more complex supply networks, greater transport choice, and less reliable transit times. Quick-response and JIT depend on the opposite: short lead times, fast replenishment, and low inventory. The two strategies are structurally in tension.

Q3 — What is the difference between logistics and supply chain management?

Logistics focuses on the efficient flow and storage of goods and information from source to consumption. SCM is broader — it includes suppliers, internal functions, logistics activities, and customers in one integrated system, with greater emphasis on cross-organizational strategic coordination.

Limits & Failure Points
Limits & Failure Points
TLC Failure
When TLC Breaks Down

TLC fails in practice when companies still operate in departmental silos, when managers chase local performance targets instead of total system performance, or when the firm lacks the information systems needed to see real trade-offs across functions.

JIT & Global Limits
When JIT Becomes Unsustainable

JIT becomes increasingly fragile as supply chains lengthen and transit reliability drops. Global sourcing adds uncertainty that JIT cannot absorb without disruption. COVID-era supply chain breakdowns illustrated this at scale.

Scope of Ch.1–2:

These chapters introduce the logic of integration, planning levels, and trade-offs. They do not yet provide quantitative tools for network design, inventory calculation, or route optimisation — those come later.
Understanding why these frameworks have limits is part of understanding them. Know the conditions under which they work — and when they don't.
