What International Economics Studies

  • Studies economic interaction between sovereign states — not simply regions inside a single country
  • The underlying economics is the same, but nations can independently set tariffs, use different currencies, and pursue separate policies
  • Core concerns: trade, international payments, exchange rates, policy coordination, and capital flows
Why it matters

These independent policy choices are precisely what make international economics a distinct discipline — and what make trade so fascinating to study.

Main Review

What Students Often Miss

  • Gains from trade — asks why trade can benefit both sides, even when one country is more efficient at everything
  • Pattern of trade — asks who trades what with whom, and why specialization emerges
  • How much trade — is really about limits, barriers, and the politics of protectionism
  • The last four themes connect trade to money, finance, and the coordination of economic policy between nations
Common Correction

International economics is not only about imports and exports — money, exchange rates, capital flows, and policy all belong to the same field.

Likely Misunderstanding

Trade is not predicted mainly by "friendship," diplomatic ties, or policy agreements alone. GDP and distance do most of the basic predictive work in the model — everything else is adjustment.

Main Review

Why Distance & Borders Still Matter

  • Distance raises costs — freight, insurance, communication delays, and coordination overhead all increase with separation
  • Nearby countries trade more — proximity creates familiarity, shared infrastructure, and cultural ties that facilitate exchange
  • Borders reduce trade even when formal tariffs are low — differing regulations, standards, languages, and legal systems all add friction
  • Trade agreements help, but they cannot erase geography or dissolve national institutions
Correction

Globalization did not make distance irrelevant. Empirical research consistently finds that distance still has a large, statistically significant negative effect on trade volumes.

How the Amount of World Trade Changed

Pre–WWI
Trade expanded strongly during the first great age of globalization — falling transport costs and the gold standard supported growing exchange
1914 – 1945
War, the Great Depression, and a wave of protectionism (Smoot-Hawley era) caused world trade to collapse dramatically
Post–WWII Recovery
Slow reconstruction under GATT, Bretton Woods institutions, and gradual tariff reduction brought trade back
Late 20th–21st C.
Trade surged sharply — driven by containerization, global supply chains, digital coordination, and WTO membership expansion
Key Point

There were two major ages of globalization — not one smooth upward trend. History shows that trade can reverse.

How the Composition of Trade Changed

  • Earlier trade relied heavily on primary products — agricultural commodities, raw materials, and natural resources
  • Modern trade is dominated by manufactured goods — machinery, electronics, vehicles, and complex industrial products
  • Services have become increasingly important, enabled by improved telecommunications, the internet, and digital trade platforms
  • Developing countries have shifted from exporting mostly primary goods to exporting a broad range of manufactured products — dramatically changing their place in world trade

Direct Answers

  • The 7 recurring themes: gains from trade · pattern of trade · protectionism/how much trade · balance of payments · exchange rate determination · international policy coordination · international capital market
  • Two strongest predictors of trade volume: the size of both economies (GDP) and the geographic distance between them

Use these ideas to explain global trade patterns, forecast shifts in specialization, and assess country-level economic risk

Add-On

Where the Ideas Don't Fully Explain

  • The gravity model predicts broad patterns well — but not perfectly; outliers exist and require additional explanation
  • Special factors — shared language, colonial history, multinational firms, and regional geography — can push trade above or below the model's prediction
  • "The world got smaller" is only partly true: distance and borders retain large, measurable effects on trade even today
  • Chapters 1–2 introduce patterns and regularities, but do not yet fully explain comparative advantage, specialization, or all modern trade outcomes — those come later
Looking Ahead

The gravity model describes what we observe. The theories of comparative advantage and factor endowments (Heckscher-Ohlin) explain why these patterns arise — that's where the course goes next.
